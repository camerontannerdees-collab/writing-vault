I love the sentiment "AI won't take your job, but someone using AI well will." What it gets right is that the threat isn't technological, it's human. The person who figures out how to build faster, automate the repeatable, and move on to the next problem is the competition. Not the software.

What most people miss is how much the barrier to building has dropped. In any field, the gap between identifying a problem and having a working solution used to require developers, project cycles, and budget approval. Now a single person willing to iterate can go from problem to working tool in a week. That's true in customer success, in operations, in marketing, in healthcare. The specific domain doesn't change the dynamic.

In my own work I've watched it play out directly. Meeting recaps that go out the same day, grounded in a shared database of product capabilities. Customer health profiles built and updated without manual entry. Slide decks prepped before calls. None of it required a developer. It required someone willing to sit with a problem long enough to build something. The teams compounding that kind of output month over month aren't moving faster because they have better tools. They're moving faster because they decided to use them.
