The debate about AI replacing jobs is missing the more interesting question. Whether your role survives isn't the point. What's changing is how fast a small team can identify a problem, build a solution, and ship it. That window used to be measured in quarters. Now it's days.

I've watched this play out in customer success. A year ago, meeting follow-ups were inconsistent, action items got buried, and CSMs were spending mental energy during calls thinking about what they'd have to write afterward. We built a meeting recap system grounded in a shared database of our product capabilities and company goals, so every follow-up is standardized, action items are pulled automatically, and the output ties back to what we actually do and what we're trying to achieve. The team stays fully present in the conversation. The build took a week, not a quarter, and when something isn't working we refine it on the fly. We also built tools to track customer health profiles, prep slide decks, and surface account history before calls. None of it required a developer. It required someone willing to iterate.

That's what people are underestimating. AI doesn't eliminate the need for good judgment, relationships, or domain knowledge. What it eliminates is the gap between knowing what needs to be built and having something working. Teams that treat AI as infrastructure, something they build on rather than just chat with, are already moving to the next problem while everyone else is still solving the last one. The reluctant adopter isn't losing their job to AI. They're losing ground to the team next to them that automated the repeatable work six months ago and has been compounding that advantage ever since.
