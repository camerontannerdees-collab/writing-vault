# Writing Guide

The default style for everything written in this folder. Read this before drafting anything substantive. Override per-piece when the work calls for it, but never override silently — say what you're doing differently and why.

## Voice

Analytical and measured. Careful, data-grounded, restrained. The reader should finish a paragraph and feel like the writer thought about every sentence, not like they were carried along by momentum.

This is not academic writing. It is not corporate writing. It is the voice of an operator who reads a lot, writes carefully, and would rather be precise than impressive.

Tells of the wrong voice:
- Sounds excited about its own ideas
- Sounds excited about anything, really
- Uses three adjectives where one would do
- Hedges with "perhaps" and "arguably" to sound thoughtful
- Ends paragraphs with a flourish or a rhetorical question

Tells of the right voice:
- Short declarative sentences when the point is clear
- Longer sentences when the point genuinely needs them
- Numbers and specifics where vague claims would be easier
- Concedes the limits of what's being argued, in passing, without making a thing of it

## Structure

**Openings are flexible.** Match the piece. Do not default to a formula. The right opening for one essay is a number; for another, it's a scene; for another, it's the question being answered. Pick the one that earns the reader's attention without performing for it.

**Paragraphs stay short.** Three to five sentences in most cases. If a paragraph runs longer, it should be doing real work — a sustained argument or a careful build — not just continuing.

**One idea per paragraph.** When a second idea shows up, that's a new paragraph.

**Section headers when they help the reader, not when they help the writer.** Skip them entirely for short pieces.

**Endings don't wrap up.** They land. The last sentence should feel earned, not summative. "In conclusion" and its cousins are off-limits.

## Banned punctuation

- **Em dashes** — out. Use a period, a comma, or parentheses instead.
- **Emojis** — out. Anywhere.
- **Exclamation points** — out unless inside a quotation.
- **Semicolons** — use sparingly. They almost always indicate a sentence that should have been two sentences.

## Banned vocabulary

The full AI-tell set. None of these should appear:

- leverage, utilize
- game-changer, revolutionary, disruptive
- at the end of the day, moving forward, going forward
- it's important to note, it's worth mentioning, it's worth noting
- delve, navigate, unleash, unlock
- robust, seamless, holistic, synergy
- in today's fast-paced world, in an ever-changing landscape
- dive in, dive deep, deep dive

When tempted to use one, the better move is almost always to be more specific. "Leverage our partnerships" → name the partnerships and what they do. "Navigate the challenges" → name the challenges. The buzzword is hiding the work.

## Other rules

**Numbers belong where numbers belong.** If a sentence makes a quantitative claim, the sentence should contain the number. "Most of the team" is weaker than "twelve of fourteen people." "A lot of revenue" is weaker than "$2.4M."

**Concrete nouns over abstract ones.** "The product" is better than "the offering." "The customer" is better than "the stakeholder."

**Active voice unless passive is genuinely better.** It usually isn't.

**No throat-clearing.** Cut "I think," "I believe," "in my view" unless the distinction between your view and someone else's is the point of the sentence.

**No false balance.** If the evidence points one way, say so. Don't manufacture a counterargument to seem fair.

**Cite sources inline.** When a claim comes from a source file in `source-material/`, reference the file path. The reader and the agent should both be able to trace any claim back to where it came from.

## Tone calibration by audience

The voice stays analytical and measured regardless of audience, but a few things shift:

- **Public writing.** More room to be opinionated and voice-forward. Sharper takes. Less hedging. Assume an intelligent reader who is not in the room with you.
- **Investor and board comms.** Tighter. More numbers. Lead with the bottom line. Skip the scene-setting.
- **Team comms.** Warmer at the edges, same precision in the middle. Acknowledge people doing the work, by name, where it fits.
- **Customer comms.** Plainspoken. Cut jargon ruthlessly. Lead with what changes for them.

## Anti-AI tells (learned from feedback)

**No reframe openers.** "The debate about X is missing the real question" and "Everyone is focused on X — the real question is Y" are off-limits. They are among the most common AI constructions on LinkedIn and in thought-leadership writing. Start mid-thought, with a number, with a scene, or with the observation itself. Never with a meta-comment positioning against what others are getting wrong.

**Cap the contrast structure.** Not X, but Y is useful once. Twice is intentional. Three or more in a single piece and it becomes a detectable pattern. If a third Not X / but Y appears, restructure one of the sentences instead.

**Endings observe, they don't package.** A closing line should feel like the natural last thing to say, not a crafted takeaway. If it reads like a pull quote or a LinkedIn punchline, it is too clean. The test: would a smart person actually say this out loud, or does it only work written down? Land on an observation. Do not land on a slogan.

## What to do when this guide doesn't cover something

Ask. Or make a call and flag that it's a call so the user can correct it. Corrections that come back become new entries in this guide or feedback files in the memory folder.

## How this file grows

This is the starter version. Every time the user corrects a phrasing or a structural choice that should apply broadly, the agent should suggest adding it here. The file should be longer in three months than it is today.
